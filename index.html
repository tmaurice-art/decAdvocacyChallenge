Average lift by shop

“Lift” is the extra cars per day when Patrick is present versus absent, using long-run averages in this sample.

Ranking by lift in this dataset: 4, 2, 5, 1, 3 (highest to lowest). That ordering is the backbone of a productivity-first calendar: overweight high-lift shops unless there is a deliberate non-productivity reason to be elsewhere.

How stable is that ranking?

Bootstrap resampling (3,000 runs) gives an approximate 90% range for each shop’s lift. Wide intervals mean the sample is still noisy—especially where there are few “boss present” days.

Shops 2 and 4 have only five “present” days each in this file, so their averages can move if one week was unusual. Shops 1, 3, and 5 have more present-day rows and look somewhat tighter, but this is still a finite window—not a guarantee for all future seasons.

Written report for Patrick

Purpose. This note turns the charts above into a single decision-facing summary: where your time moves the needle most, how sure we can be, and what a few simple calendar rules imply for total cars across all shops.

What the data shows. Across 250 days, your being on site is associated with more cars fixed everywhere, but the gain is largest at Shop 4 (on the order of thirteen extra cars per day versus absent days in this sample), then Shop 2 (~ten), Shop 5 (~eight), Shop 1 (~seven), and Shop 3 (~three). Shop 3—your brother’s—is still better when you visit, but the marginal productivity gain is the smallest of the five. For fleet-wide thinking, a day spent at Shop 4 lines up with the highest expected total cars given how large its jump is while other shops stay near their “you’re elsewhere” baselines.

Recommendations (productivity-first). Bias on-site days toward Shop 4, keep Shop 2 and Shop 5 in regular rotation, use Shop 1 as needed, and treat Shop 3 as the lowest scheduling priority when the goal is maximizing cars fixed—not because Shop 3 is failing, but because the data says the incremental return to your presence is smaller there. If you use a rough figure such as $200 revenue per car, a sustained lift on the scale seen at Shop 4 translates to materially higher revenue on “you’re there” days before costs; the exact dollars should use your real pricing.

Confidence. Two hundred fifty days is enough to read direction and rough ranking, not to freeze every average as permanent truth. Uncertainty is largest for lifts driven by very few present-day observations (notably Shops 2 and 4 in this extract). Treat the ranking as a strong default; refresh after staffing changes, major accounts, or seasonality shifts.

Scenarios and caveats. The scenario comparison is illustrative: it assumes historical means continue to hold and ignores richer dynamics. It still clarifies the tradeoff: brother-heavy allocation to Shop 3 costs expected fleet cars versus leaning into Shop 4.
